Course Description

Modern AI systems (like large language models) are incredibly powerful but also mysteriously opaque: they work astonishingly well, yet even the engineers who build them don't fully understand what the billions of artificial "neurons" are actually doing inside. This class focuses on post-hoc interpretability, trying to understand trained models, like a biologist studying a complex organism. Instead of simplifying the model to make it human-readable, we will probe existing neural networks to uncover what internal structures, features, or "concepts" they've learned, and how those parts combine to produce behavior.

We will form interdisciplinary teams consisting of a non-CS PhD student, a CS PhD student with a background in machine learning, and a BauLab member who is an expert in interpretability to collaborate on a research project.

Course Goals

The aim of this class is to study how LLMs encode non-CS concepts, an area often neglected in interpretability research, and to produce publication-quality research papers suitable for submission to machine learning venues (NeurIPS, ICLR, ICML conferences or workshops).

Teams will conduct original research following the complete workflow: from concept selection and exploration, through systematic application of interpretability methods, to writing and peer review. The timeline is ambitious—some teams may target NeurIPS 2026 (May deadline), while others may polish their work over the summer for later venues. Both paths represent successful outcomes.

Success metrics: Rigorous investigation with proper controls and validation. Negative results with careful analysis are just as publishable as positive findings. The focus is on research quality, not necessarily acceptance to a specific venue.

Pre-requisites

Familiarity with linear algebra, probability, calculus, deep learning, Python, and PyTorch is recommended. Understanding transformer architecture and having experience building them from scratch is a plus.
